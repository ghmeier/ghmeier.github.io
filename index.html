Senior Design

Expresso

  • Role: Team Lead
  • Team Members: 5
  • Project Plan
  • Design Document
  • Website

    For senior design, my group proposed Expresso, and automated, local, coffee subscription service. We’ve decided to build pursue Expresso for our senior design project because it presents interesting technical challenges, opportunities for learning new software engineering concepts, and the ability to influence people positively. Since Expresso aims to automate the process of ordering and shipping at every point except actually sending the packages, we have to build a reliable logistics systems for consumers and providers. We’ll be relying on partnering with local roasters to produce coffee for subscribers which creates a multiple stakeholders to please with the system, adding another layer of technicality. As a group, we wanted to grapple with a new challenge that wasn’t a part of our previous coursework, so we decided to build Expresso with a distributed, microservice architecture to give us experience designing a common software architecture from the ground up.

    Finally, Expresso helps local coffee roasters scale their business more quickly and cost effectively than they could otherwise. By giving local roasters the opportunity to use Expresso as a shipping platform rather than having to build their own or contract it out, we’ve freed up roasters to do what they do best, roast coffee. Given that these roasters are oftentimes local businesses, committed to community growth, we think this service can have a positive impact on local businesses at the heart of their communities.

Software Engineering Reflection

I made my choice to attend Iowa State in part due to the presence of a Software Engineering program. I specifically wanted to learn the skills of applying knowledge of software systems to practical, real world problems. After spending four years in the SE program at ISU, I've learned many of the basic principles of both Computer Science and Software Engineering. Without the formal learning in courses at ISU, I would be far less prepared to tackle the tough problems when designing complicated software processes and systems. Specifically, the wide range of assignments, from individual, focused projects to larger, group oriented systems, gave me great experience in building those sorts of systems in a professional setting. My time at internships and working on side projects has been most beneficial to formulate and solve real engineering problems. This year, I've been building open source registration software for hackathons since I saw a real need, and knew how to scope the problem and build a useable solution. While the software engineering coursework didn't directly contribute to understanding engineering in a global context, my general education and personal interest definitely put the engineering work I'll be doing in perspective. At a more fundamental level, the use of groups in ISU's SE curriculum gave me great experience in coordinating and managing a team of people. As team lead on many of my projects, I was able to get a taste of what being a more senior engineer professionally would feel like and what sort of responsibilities it would entail.

If I were to re-structure my undergraduate experience and take it again, I wouldn't take different courses that are currently available, but I would hope that the courses I did take which focused on project management and system design were more current and well structured. I felt like those courses were most lacking in the curriculum, and I was forced to do much of my learning outside class and on the job rather than having a base of fundamental learning from coursework.
